Hyphenated ~ Negotiating Identities as an Asian-Canadian

Published by admin on February 10, 2018February 10, 2018

by Kana Shishikura

In September, 2017, my research team and I, under the Asian Pathways Research Lab, published our first magazine called the Pathways, as part of the Grandparent Project: Inter-generational Conversations about Family, Mobility and Identity.
The aim of the project was to understand and generate meaningful conversations about the changing meanings of home, belonging, mobility, identity, diaspora and citizenship for each generation, at different periods of time and environment. Students who participated in the project conducted ethnographic research and collected mobility histories of their own family members. As the final product, students wrote an article on the their family’s mobilities, life trajectories and pathways.

 

At our publication launch party with my research peers. Our magazine, “Pathways”, shown in the centre

For the Grandparent Project, I wrote an article on my brother-in-law, Joe. I was curious to learn about Joe’s lived experiences growing up in Canada as the son of immigrant parents from the Philippines. My article explores a couple of overarching themes: the language transmission in Joe’s household, the ways in which he navigated his racial and ethnic identities through his daily life in Brampton, Ontario as a kid, and how his recent journey to Ho Chi Minh City in Vietnam as an international school teacher and his mobility have impacted his identity reconfiguration. Joe’s life stories and his mobility speak to the complexity of identity formation and the intimate connection between race and space engendered through local and global racial hierarchies.

Although my article, as well as my peer’s, will be posted online in the near future, I would like to share parts of my article about Joe’s life stories and the complex, yet sophisticated methods he used to navigate his ‘hyphenated’ identity as an ‘Asian-Canadian.’

“Hyphenated – Negotiating Identities as an Asian-Canadian”
Kana Shishikura
September, 2017

Joe was born in 1986 and raised in Brampton, Ontario, with two older sisters. Both of his parents were born in the Philippines and immigrated to Canada during the early 1970s.

Joe when he was a kid – at his parent’s place

While Joe was growing up in Brampton, Ontario, the population in his community was predominantly of European descent. Joe was one of two kids of Asian descent in his grade and he remembers being teased by his white classmates and feeling like an “outsider”. The racialization he faced as a visible minority occurred on a daily basis. Being one of the very few kids of Asian descent in school, his “Asian” ethnicity came to represent those very broad conceptions of what it was to be an Asian. He was “the Asian kid” at school that people knew about, resulting in tokenism of his identity.

However, even superficial acceptances and friendships based on forms of misrecognition with his classmates was more important to him than standing up for himself. Having experienced overt racism through his daily interactions, and wishing to be accepted, Joe remembers playing into his classmates’ racist “jokes” about his “Asian” appearance and culture (for example, “jokes” on his relatively thinner eyes). Ultimately, he became “whitewashed” to adapt and navigate through the dominant culture at school. Regardless of his efforts to fit in, Joe struggled with accepting his racial difference. He recalls feeling alienated and even regretful and ashamed about being Filipino.

<English as Survival Mechanism>
Joe’s parents were also aware of the consequences of being a visible minority in their community and took sophisticated approaches to prevent further racism and racial misrecognition of Joe and his two sisters.

Despite Tagalog being their mother tongue, Joe’s parents preferred to use English at home with the family. According to Joe, his parents believed that learning Tagalog would result in funny accents in Joe’s and his sisters’ English and considered it to be a source of difference or discrimination from their white peers. Thus, Joe grew up speaking English to his siblings and parents, and “Taglish,” which is a mix of English and Tagalog. Speaking English at home reflected his parents’ efforts to prevent further racism of Joe and his two sisters and to blend in with the community in which they lived.

Reflecting on the language preference in his household, Joe does not regret nor resent the approach that his parents pursued. However, Joe now wishes he could communicate more in Tagalog, given his new life in Vietnam as a high school teacher at an international school, where some of the students are Filipino.

<“But I am Canadian.”>
Joe received his Bachelor degree in Communications, English and Professional Writing from the University of Toronto Mississauga campus, as well as a degree in Bachelor of Education from Ontario Institute of Studies in Education (OISE).  Becoming a teacher was always his dream, and after completing his second degree, Joe began his career as a teacher in the Toronto District School Board. After marrying my oldest sister in July 2016, Joe decided to continue his career abroad and moved to Ho Chi Minh City in Vietnam by himself. The life that awaited him in Vietnam was way more different than what he had expected.

In Vietnam, Joe also faces the reality of his ‘in-betweenness’ or his mixed identity as a Canadian of Filipino descent. Canadian people are usually imagined as white, European looking individuals in Vietnam. This racialized geographical imagination of Canada situates Joe in an ambiguous ‘in-between’ category of people. The identity as Canadian that Joe has struggled to navigate and embraced all his life was all of a sudden being questioned in a foreign land.

Joe often experiences the exoticization of his nationality, ethnicity, and identity in Vietnam, a familiar circumstance that he experienced in his community back in Brampton, Ontario. The sense of alienation and negotiation of his identity is a continuing process even in a different landscape.

“Where are you from?” is a common question that Joe gets. Vietnamese people are often confused by his introduction as a Canadian person when he visibly does not look like a Caucasian foreign, but rather more like themselves. With the combination of his unaccented native English, Vietnamese people are even more perplexed by this Vietnamese-looking, but Canadian persona.

Joe on his motorbike, in a busy street in Ho Chi Minh City, Vietnam

The recognition and acceptance of Joe as Canadian in Vietnam seems to be contingent on him also identifying himself as Filipino. By announcing himself as “Filipino-Canadian” local Vietnamese people finally comprehend Joe as a Canadian individual. While Joe’s hyphenated identity may play an important role in Vietnam in defying and challenging the hegemonic discourse of what Canadians look like, this hyphenation also produces differences between his “identity” and “identification.” “No matter the location, I am Canadian. Home is always Canada,” says Joe, clearly identifying himself as Canadian, without adding the hyphenated ethnicity. On the other hand, when in Vietnam, the hegemonic imagination of Canada and “Canadians” as predominantly being Western Europeans, especially, British and French, the hyphenated Canadians become situated at the periphery within the framework of “Canadian.”

Throughout his life, in both Brampton and Ho Chi Minh City Joe faced overt forms of local racisms.  He was forced to constantly learn how to navigate his racialized environment and present his racialized identity in sophisticated ways. In Canada, he played along with the racist jokes in grade school and spoke English with no accent in order to mitigate racial misrecognition and racism. In Vietnam, Joe also utilizes the English language as a tool and coping mechanism to reduce racism, and to shift the local racial misunderstanding of him in Ho Chi Minh City.

Joe’s experiences in in Canada and Vietnam, has challenged and helped him develop his cultural and racial elasticity through the constant navigation, negotiation and presentation of his identity in different locations. Joe’s racialized body is read differently in different geographical locations and he is constantly re-negotiating and re-configuring his identity in new landscapes.

——————————-

Me and Joe in September 2014, during my move-in day at Woodsworth Residence

Joe’s lived experiences in Canada and Vietnam demonstrate how identity formation can be situational, relational and geographically sensitive. His identity is very complex, intersectional and ambiguous, but this is not a rare identity configuration in Canada. Many immigrants in Canada embody these ambiguities. These complexities make them unique and tell a mobility story that has never been told.

Written By Mayu Kawada . . . 「趣味は写真を撮ることです。」と言う人は数多くいます。実際、私もその一人です。友達といる時、旅行に行った時、その一瞬を切り取る「写真」は文章よりもずっと雄弁で、大切な思い出を残してくれます。また、インスタグラムやフェイスブックなどソーシャルメディアが発達している今に「写真」は欠かせない要素だと思います。 しかし、私がもっとも楽しみとすることは撮るだけではなく、撮った後の「編集」という作業です。写真は自分が思っている通り簡単に撮れるものではなく、色々な悪条件（天候、光の具合）などにより、実際に自分の目で捉えたものと同じ状態のものを写すのは意外と難しいです。それを修正することにより、より思い出深く、自分好みの一枚に変えることができます。この記事では、あくまで自己流ではありますが、私がどんなアプリを使ってどのように写真を編集しているのかを紹介しようと思います。 まず、編集には『Lightroom』というアプリを使います。 ”Photoshop”も編集をするのによく使うと言いますが、Lightroomの方がシンプルかつ簡単なので私はこちらを使用しています。 携帯・パソコンどちらからもダウンロードできますが、携帯の場合は無料、パソコンの場合は有料になります。ここでは携帯を使った上で説明していきたいと思います。 編集する写真はこちらです。観光名所の一つ、京都の伏見稲荷で撮った一枚ですが、鳥居が連なっているため、影が多くとても暗いのに気づきます。下の写真を様々な機能を使って、鮮やかな写真に編集していきたいと思います。 写真を編集する時、私は5つのステップ、Crop → Exposure → Contrast →  Saturation → Color の順に修正していきます。特にこだわりはないので順番は入れ替えても大丈夫です！ CROP （切り取り） 写真を切り取り、目立たせたい人物、または物を中心に持ってくる作業です。例の写真では、人物が少し左寄りなので、右部分を動かし左右の幅が大体同じスペースになるように調節します。Lightroomでは切り取りの際、写真を均等にするため下のように線が見えるので是非活用してください。 EXPOSURE （露出） ”Light”の枠で明るさを調整します。写真が明るい場合は露出を下げ、暗い場合は露出を上げます。この写真の場合、影が理由で浴衣の色や肌の色が大分暗いため露出を上げ、明るさを足します。どのくらい上げるかは自分の感覚を頼りに微調節で決めますが、点を動かし元との違いをはっきり確かめることで丁度良い明るさがより定められるようになります。（写真の場合は+0.92） CONTRAST （コントラスト） 次に行うのが明暗差を変える作業、コントラストです。変え方はとてもシンプルで + を押せば明暗差がよりくっきり、- を押せば柔らかい印象にすることが可能です。光と影を強調すれば、画像の雰囲気をガラッと変えられるため、この昨日は写真を編集する上で必須です！また、主にポートレートや人の写真はコントラストを強くし、背景などの写真の場合はコントラストを低くする場合が多いです。（+40にしたことで後ろ姿がよりはっきりと見えます） Read more…
